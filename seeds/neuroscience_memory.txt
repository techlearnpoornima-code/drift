Neuroscience: Memory and Learning — Reference Document

1. TYPES OF MEMORY: EXPLICIT AND IMPLICIT

Memory is broadly divided into explicit (declarative) and implicit (non-declarative) systems. Explicit memory requires conscious recall and encompasses facts and events that can be verbally reported. Implicit memory operates without conscious awareness and includes skills, habits, priming, and conditioned responses. This distinction is not merely conceptual but reflects different neural systems: explicit memory depends heavily on the medial temporal lobe, while implicit memory engages the basal ganglia, cerebellum, and amygdala depending on the memory type. Within explicit memory, a further division separates episodic memory — the recollection of personally experienced events tied to a specific time and place — from semantic memory, which stores general world knowledge and facts independent of personal experience. A person may recall that Paris is the capital of France (semantic) without remembering when or how they learned it, while remembering their first visit to Paris constitutes an episodic memory.

2. THE HIPPOCAMPUS AND DECLARATIVE MEMORY FORMATION

The hippocampus, located in the medial temporal lobe, is essential for the formation of new explicit memories. The most influential evidence comes from the case of Henry Molaison, known in the literature as HM, who underwent bilateral hippocampal resection in 1953 to treat severe epilepsy. Following surgery, Molaison was unable to form new declarative memories — a condition called anterograde amnesia — yet his memories from before the surgery remained largely intact, and his implicit learning abilities were preserved. This dissociation established that the hippocampus is required for encoding new declarative memories but is not the permanent storage site for remote memories. It also demonstrated that implicit and explicit memory systems operate independently. Molaison could learn new motor skills, such as mirror drawing, improving with practice while having no conscious recollection of ever performing the task.

3. LONG-TERM POTENTIATION: CELLULAR MECHANISM OF MEMORY

Long-term potentiation (LTP) is the primary cellular mechanism underlying synaptic strengthening and is considered the physiological basis of learning and memory. LTP was first described by Timothy Bliss and Terje Lømo in 1973 in studies of the rabbit hippocampus. The induction of LTP requires the activation of NMDA (N-methyl-D-aspartate) receptors, which are voltage-dependent and ligand-gated. NMDA receptors are blocked by magnesium ions at resting potential; simultaneous presynaptic activity and postsynaptic depolarisation removes this block, allowing calcium influx. This calcium entry triggers a cascade of intracellular signalling that results in the insertion of additional AMPA receptors into the postsynaptic membrane, increasing the sensitivity of the synapse to subsequent glutamate release. The result is a long-lasting increase in synaptic strength between the neurons involved.

4. SYNAPTIC PLASTICITY AND HEBB'S RULE

Synaptic plasticity refers to the capacity of synapses to strengthen or weaken over time in response to changes in activity. The theoretical framework for activity-dependent synaptic change was proposed by Donald Hebb in 1949: neurons that fire together wire together. This principle, often called Hebb's rule, predicts that a synapse will be strengthened when a presynaptic neuron repeatedly contributes to firing a postsynaptic neuron. The converse also holds: synaptic connections can be weakened through long-term depression (LTD), which occurs when presynaptic activity is not consistently paired with postsynaptic depolarisation. Synaptic plasticity underlies not only memory formation but also developmental refinement of neural circuits.

5. MEMORY CONSOLIDATION: SYNAPTIC AND SYSTEMS LEVELS

Consolidation is the process by which newly acquired memories are stabilised. It operates at two distinct levels. Synaptic consolidation occurs within hours of learning and involves protein synthesis at the synapse; inhibiting protein synthesis in this window impairs long-term memory. Systems consolidation operates over weeks to years, during which memories initially dependent on the hippocampus are gradually transferred to the neocortex for long-term storage. Sleep plays a critical role in both forms of consolidation. During slow-wave sleep, sharp-wave ripples in the hippocampus are thought to replay recent experiences, facilitating transfer to cortical regions. REM sleep is associated with consolidation of procedural and emotional memories. Sleep deprivation impairs memory consolidation across multiple memory types.

6. NEUROPLASTICITY AND ADULT NEUROGENESIS

The brain retains the capacity to reorganise its structure and function in response to experience throughout life — a property termed neuroplasticity. Contrary to the long-held belief that adult brains cannot generate new neurons, adult neurogenesis has been confirmed in the dentate gyrus of the hippocampus in mammals, including humans. New granule cells are continuously produced in this region and are thought to contribute to pattern separation — the ability to distinguish between similar memories. Stress and glucocorticoids suppress hippocampal neurogenesis, while physical exercise and environmental enrichment promote it.

7. THE AMYGDALA AND EMOTIONAL MEMORY

The amygdala, an almond-shaped structure in the medial temporal lobe, modulates the encoding and consolidation of emotionally significant memories. During fear conditioning, the amygdala associates a neutral stimulus with an aversive outcome, and this association can be retained after a single pairing. Stress hormones, particularly adrenaline (epinephrine) and cortisol, enhance memory encoding for emotional events by acting on the basolateral amygdala, which in turn projects to the hippocampus and increases consolidation. This explains why emotionally arousing events are typically remembered more vividly and durably than neutral ones. The amygdala does not store the declarative content of a memory but regulates its strength.

8. FORGETTING: THE EBBINGHAUS CURVE AND MECHANISMS OF MEMORY LOSS

Hermann Ebbinghaus conducted systematic experiments on memory in the 1880s, quantifying the rate at which learned material is forgotten over time. His forgetting curve shows that memory loss is most rapid in the first hours after learning and then levels off. Crucially, forgetting is not simply the passive decay of memory traces. Two active mechanisms contribute substantially: interference, where competing memories disrupt retrieval (proactive interference from older memories and retroactive interference from newer ones), and retrieval failure, where a memory remains stored but cannot be accessed without an appropriate cue. Context-dependent and state-dependent memory effects illustrate that retrieval is sensitive to cues present at encoding. Forgetting therefore reflects partly the limits of retrieval processes, not only the degradation of stored representations.
