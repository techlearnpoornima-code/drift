Dopamine and the Reward System

Dopamine is a monoamine neurotransmitter synthesised primarily in the substantia nigra and
the ventral tegmental area (VTA). It plays a central role in the mesolimbic pathway, which
connects the VTA to the nucleus accumbens and is the principal circuit underlying reward
anticipation and motivation.

Dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens signals reward prediction error — the difference
between expected and received reward. When an outcome exceeds expectation, dopamine neurons
increase their firing rate. When an outcome falls below expectation, firing rate drops below
baseline. This mechanism was first described by Wolfram Schultz and colleagues in primate
studies published in 1997.

The prefrontal cortex receives dopaminergic projections via the mesocortical pathway. These
projections modulate working memory, executive function, and impulse control. Dopamine
operates on an inverted-U dose-response curve in the prefrontal cortex: too little impairs
function, and too much also impairs it. This is why both dopamine deficiency and excess
produce cognitive dysfunction.

Chronic stress reduces dopamine synthesis and depletes available dopamine in the nucleus
accumbens. This reduction is associated with anhedonia — the inability to experience pleasure
from previously rewarding activities — which is a core symptom of major depressive disorder.

Parkinson's disease results from the progressive loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia
nigra. The motor symptoms of Parkinson's — tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia — emerge when
approximately 60 to 80 percent of these neurons have been lost. The standard pharmacological
treatment is levodopa (L-DOPA), a dopamine precursor that crosses the blood-brain barrier,
where it is converted to dopamine.

Addiction involves the dysregulation of dopamine signalling. Repeated exposure to addictive
substances causes downregulation of dopamine D2 receptors in the striatum, reducing the
sensitivity of the reward circuit to natural rewards and driving compulsive drug-seeking
behaviour. This receptor downregulation has been documented in studies using PET imaging in
humans with substance use disorder.

Serotonin is a separate monoamine neurotransmitter synthesised primarily in the raphe nuclei
of the brainstem. Unlike dopamine, serotonin's primary role is in mood regulation, sleep,
and appetite — not in reward prediction. Confusing serotonin and dopamine pathways is a
common error: SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) act on serotonin, not on
dopamine, and have minimal direct effect on the reward circuit.

Oxytocin is a peptide hormone and neuropeptide produced in the hypothalamus and released by
the posterior pituitary. It is associated with social bonding, trust, and maternal behaviour.
Oxytocin is not a monoamine and does not function as a reward-prediction signal; attributing
dopamine's reward functions to oxytocin is factually incorrect.

Key facts summary:
- Dopamine is synthesised in the substantia nigra and VTA.
- The mesolimbic pathway (VTA to nucleus accumbens) underlies reward anticipation.
- Reward prediction error is encoded by dopamine firing rate changes.
- Chronic stress depletes dopamine and causes anhedonia.
- Parkinson's disease involves loss of substantia nigra dopaminergic neurons.
- Levodopa is the primary pharmacological treatment for Parkinson's.
- Addiction causes D2 receptor downregulation in the striatum.
- Serotonin, not dopamine, is the primary target of SSRIs.
- Oxytocin is a peptide hormone, not a monoamine reward signal.
