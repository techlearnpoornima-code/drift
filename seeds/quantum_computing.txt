Quantum Computing: Physical Principles and Current State

1. QUBITS AND QUANTUM STATES

A qubit is the fundamental unit of quantum information. Unlike a classical bit, which is
either 0 or 1, a qubit can exist in a superposition of both states simultaneously. This
superposition is described by a quantum state |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩, where α and β are
complex amplitudes satisfying |α|² + |β|² = 1. Measurement collapses the qubit to
either |0⟩ with probability |α|² or |1⟩ with probability |β|².

Superposition is a property of the qubit while unmeasured. Measurement irreversibly
destroys the superposition — this is the measurement problem and is not a technology
limitation but a fundamental feature of quantum mechanics.

2. ENTANGLEMENT

Two qubits are entangled when their quantum states cannot be described independently.
The Bell state |Φ+⟩ = (|00⟩ + |11⟩)/√2 is a maximally entangled two-qubit state.
Measuring one qubit instantly determines the state of the other, regardless of physical
separation. This correlation is non-local but cannot be used to transmit information
faster than light — this is established by the no-communication theorem.

Entanglement is a resource for quantum computation and quantum communication. It is
destroyed by decoherence (unwanted interaction with the environment).

3. QUANTUM GATES

Quantum computation proceeds by applying unitary transformations (gates) to qubits.
Common single-qubit gates include:
- Hadamard (H): puts a qubit into equal superposition
- Pauli-X: equivalent to a classical NOT gate
- T gate (π/8 gate): introduces a phase of e^(iπ/4)

The CNOT (controlled-NOT) gate is the standard two-qubit gate. Together with
single-qubit gates, it forms a universal gate set sufficient for any quantum computation.

Gate fidelity measures how closely a physical gate implementation matches the ideal
unitary. State-of-the-art superconducting qubits achieve single-qubit gate fidelities
above 99.9% and two-qubit gate fidelities in the range of 99.0–99.7%.

4. DECOHERENCE AND COHERENCE TIMES

Decoherence is the loss of quantum superposition due to unwanted interaction with the
environment. It is the primary obstacle to practical quantum computation.

Coherence time is quantified by two parameters:
- T1 (energy relaxation time): time for a qubit to decay from |1⟩ to |0⟩
- T2 (dephasing time): time for phase information to be lost; T2 ≤ 2×T1

Leading superconducting qubit platforms (Google, IBM, IQM) report T1 times in the
range of 100–500 microseconds and T2 times of 50–300 microseconds as of 2024.
Trapped-ion systems achieve T2 times of seconds to minutes but have slower gate
operation times of approximately 10–100 microseconds per two-qubit gate.

5. QUANTUM ERROR CORRECTION

Physical qubits are noisy. Quantum error correction (QEC) encodes one logical qubit
across multiple physical qubits to detect and correct errors without measuring the
logical state directly.

The surface code is the leading QEC scheme for superconducting qubits. It encodes one
logical qubit in a 2D lattice of physical qubits. The code distance d determines
fault tolerance: a distance-d surface code can correct up to ⌊(d−1)/2⌋ errors.

The threshold theorem states that if physical gate error rates are below a threshold
(approximately 1% for the surface code), arbitrarily long quantum computations can be
performed with exponentially low logical error rates by increasing code distance.

A commonly cited estimate is that one fault-tolerant logical qubit requires
approximately 1,000–10,000 physical qubits depending on the target logical error rate
and physical error rates. This ratio is called the qubit overhead.

6. QUANTUM ADVANTAGE AND SUPREMACY

Quantum computational advantage refers to performing a specific task faster than any
known classical algorithm. Google's 2019 Sycamore experiment claimed quantum supremacy
by sampling from a random quantum circuit in 200 seconds, a task estimated to take
10,000 years on a classical supercomputer at the time. IBM later revised this classical
simulation estimate significantly downward, and subsequent work reduced the classical
runtime further — the precise magnitude of advantage for that specific task remains
contested.

Shor's algorithm factors an N-bit integer in polynomial time O((log N)³), exponentially
faster than the best known classical algorithm. It requires a fault-tolerant quantum
computer with thousands of logical qubits to threaten RSA-2048 — a scale not achieved
as of 2024.

Grover's algorithm provides a quadratic speedup for unstructured search: searching
N items in O(√N) time versus O(N) classically. This speedup is provably optimal for
unstructured search — no quantum algorithm can do better.

7. QUBIT MODALITIES

Superconducting qubits: Based on Josephson junctions operating near absolute zero
(~15 millikelvin). Fast gate times (~10–50 nanoseconds). Short coherence times relative
to trapped ions. Dominant platform at Google, IBM, IQM, and Rigetti.

Trapped ions: Individual atomic ions (typically ytterbium or barium) held by
electromagnetic fields. Long coherence times (seconds). Slower gate operations than
superconducting qubits. Used by IonQ, Quantinuum (formerly Honeywell), and Oxford
Ionics.

Photonic qubits: Encode information in photon polarisation or path. Operate at room
temperature. Challenges include probabilistic two-qubit gates and photon loss.
PsiQuantum and Xanadu are leading photonic quantum computing companies.

Neutral atoms: Individual atoms trapped in optical tweezers. Demonstrated high-fidelity
entanglement across arrays of up to ~1,000 qubits as of 2023. QuEra Computing and
Pasqal are key companies in this space.

8. CURRENT SCALE AND LIMITATIONS

IBM's Condor processor (2023) contains 1,121 physical qubits. Utility-scale quantum
computation on problems beyond classical simulation reach has been demonstrated only
for specific, narrow tasks. General-purpose fault-tolerant quantum computing requires
millions of physical qubits — this has not been achieved.

Quantum volume is a hardware-agnostic benchmark introduced by IBM that measures the
largest square random circuit a device can execute with greater than 2/3 fidelity.
Higher quantum volume indicates better performance. It captures qubit count, connectivity,
and gate fidelity simultaneously.

The quantum computing field distinguishes three eras:
- NISQ (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum): current era; 50–1,000+ noisy qubits without
  full error correction; limited depth circuits
- Early fault-tolerant: small numbers of logical qubits with partial error correction
- Fully fault-tolerant: millions of physical qubits, full error correction, capable of
  running Shor's and other demanding algorithms
